PTQ Is Solving the Wrong Problem — My Take After Writing RDQ

Six months of chasing residual stream drift left me with some opinions I haven't fully said out loud yet. This is that post — less "here are the numbers," more "here's what I think it means."

Prateek Singh · Author, RDQ (arXiv:2607.10137)

I've now written two posts laying out what RDQ found — the mechanism, the numbers, the ablation that survives the "isn't this just AWQ" objection. This one is different. It's not a results post. It's what I actually think, having spent six months staring at residual streams, about where the quantization field is spending its effort and where I think it's misallocated.

Take this as opinion, not as a claim I can fully defend with a p-value. Some of it I'm confident in. Some of it is a hunch I'd bet on but wouldn't stake a paper on yet.

Opinion 01

We've Over-Invested in Outlier Suppression

Confidence: high

Rotation methods — QuaRot, SpinQuant — are elegant, and I said so in my last post. But look at how much of the field's attention has gone toward outlier geometry versus cross-layer error accumulation. Almost all of it is the former. GPTQ, AWQ, SmoothQuant, OmniQuant, QuaRot, SpinQuant, LeanQuant — every one of them optimizes each layer against a clean, idealized input. Not one of the widely-cited PTQ methods asks what the layer actually receives after everything upstream has already been quantized.

That's a strange blind spot for a field this mature. It's not that outliers don't matter — they clearly do. It's that we found one failure mode early (outliers are visually dramatic, easy to profile, satisfying to fix), and the field anchored on it so hard that a second, larger failure mode sat there unmeasured for years. The r=0.999 correlation between drift and log-PPL isn't a subtle effect. It was just never the thing anyone was plotting.

My honest read: if I had to bet on where the next real gains in sub-4-bit PTQ come from, it's not a better rotation matrix. It's better modeling of what happens to the stream over 32+ sequential error injections. Rotation is a one-shot geometric fix. Drift is a compounding process. Compounding processes deserve sequential fixes, not one-shot ones.

Opinion 02

The Gaussian Assumption Should Have Been Questioned a Decade Ago

Confidence: high

27 of 32 LLaMA-3-8B layers fail a KS normality test. That's not a subtle statistical technicality — it's most of the model. And yet a huge amount of PTQ theory, mine included, quietly assumes Gaussian residuals because the math is tractable when you do. My own Theorem 2 bound predicts 1.06× degradation at W3; the real number is 1.36×. The bound isn't conservative, it's wrong, in the specific sense of being violated rather than just loose.

I don't think this is a small embarrassment unique to my paper. I think it's evidence that a lot of theoretical PTQ work has been building clean proofs on a distributional assumption nobody actually checked. I checked it because I was already instrumenting the residual stream for other reasons — not because I set out looking for this. I suspect if more people ran a KS test on their calibration activations before writing a bound, we'd see a lot more "proposition" labels and a lot fewer "theorem" labels in this literature. That's not a bad thing. It's more honest.

Opinion 03

"Static Calibration" Is the Field's Quiet Bug

Confidence: medium-high

The AWQ-on-Qwen result bothers me more than it probably should. AWQ gives 8.50 PPL on Qwen-2.5-7B at W4 — worse than doing nothing (RTN gives 6.46). That's not a small gap. A well-regarded, widely-deployed method underperforming the null baseline on a mainstream model, because its salient-channel heuristic was implicitly tuned on LLaMA-family statistics and doesn't transfer.

I don't think this is really an AWQ-specific problem. I think it's evidence that most "activation-aware" methods are activation-aware of the wrong activations — clean, static, single-snapshot FP16 activations, treated as if they were architecture-invariant ground truth. CEC's fix is almost embarrassingly simple in hindsight: measure the channel importance on what the layer will really see, and it stops mattering whether your heuristic happens to transfer across architectures, because you're not relying on a transferable heuristic at all.

Opinion 04

I Don't Think RDQ Is the Endpoint — And I'm Wary of Papers That Claim to Be

Confidence: high, this one's more meta

I structured RDQ around one central number (r=0.999) because I think that's genuinely the strongest thing in the paper — but I want to be careful not to oversell it. CEC is a wrapper around AWQ-style scaling. It's compatible with GPTQ, LeanQuant, whatever comes next. I said this explicitly in the paper and I mean it: the contribution is the sequential drift-correction principle, not a specific scaling formula. If someone plugs LeanQuant's loss-aware grid into the CEC loop and beats my W3 numbers next quarter, that's not a competing result, that's the expected next step.

I think the field has a mild allergy to admitting a method is a "wrapper" rather than a self-contained breakthrough, because wrappers sound less publishable. I'd rather be right about scope than get credit for a bigger claim than the evidence supports. The Theorem-2-as-proposition decision was the same instinct — better to under-claim and be correct than to claim a tight bound I know gets violated at the bit-width that actually matters.

What I'd Actually Bet On

If I Had Another Six Months

  • 🎯
    RotRDQ, properly tested. Rotation for geometry, CEC for drift, on the same model. I've said this hypothesis out loud twice now without evidence — that's the next thing I owe this series, not more theory.
  • 🎯
    Drift-aware calibration for activation quantization, not just weights. W+A CEC is untouched in the paper. Activations see drifted inputs too, and I'd guess the effect is larger there, not smaller.
  • 🎯
    A non-Gaussian version of the perplexity bound. Not because the current one is useless — it's directionally right — but because I think a bound that doesn't assume Gaussianity is achievable and would actually hold at W3.
  • 🎯
    Checking whether this generalizes past dense transformers. MoE routing means the "layer receives drifted input" story gets more complicated — the drift itself becomes input-dependent. I don't know yet if CEC's calibration-set assumptions survive that.
Where I Land

The Honest Summary

My opinion, stated plainly: the PTQ field has spent disproportionate effort on outlier geometry because outliers are easy to see and satisfying to fix, while a larger, more predictable failure mode — cross-layer drift, correlated with perplexity almost perfectly — sat underexamined because it required instrumenting the whole forward pass instead of one weight matrix. I don't think RDQ closes that gap. I think it's the first paper to put a number on how big the gap is.

If you disagree with any of this — especially the claim that static calibration is a field-wide quiet bug rather than an AWQ-specific quirk — I'd genuinely like to hear the counter-argument. That's the kind of pushback that would actually change what I test next.
