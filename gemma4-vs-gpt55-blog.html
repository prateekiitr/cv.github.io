Gemma 4 vs GPT-5.5: Open Weights vs. Closed Power

GPT-5.5 just landed on April 23rd. Gemma 4 dropped three weeks earlier. The gap between proprietary frontier models and open-weights alternatives has never been more interesting — or more complicated — than it is right now.

Two model releases. Three weeks apart. Completely different design philosophies. And yet the comparison keeps coming up in every Slack thread, every ML team standup, and every "which model should we standardize on?" conversation I've been part of this month.

So let's do this properly. Not as a benchmark beauty pageant — those are everywhere. But as a practitioner's comparison: what each model is actually built for, where the numbers hold up, and the one question that should drive your choice: what does your deployment look like?

Context

Why This Comparison Is Genuinely Interesting

For most of 2024 and 2025, "open model vs. frontier proprietary" comparisons were a bit hollow. Open models were good but obviously trailing. The gap in reasoning, tool use, and long-context coherence was large enough that enterprise teams defaulted to GPT or Claude without much hand-wringing.

That calculus is changing. Gemma 4's 31B Dense is sitting at #3 on the Arena AI open model leaderboard, beating models 20× its size. And GPT-5.5 — which just dropped two days ago — is being called OpenAI's first fully retrained base model since GPT-4.5, with ground-up architecture changes, not just post-training iterations.

These are not incremental releases. They represent two meaningfully different bets on what "best AI" looks like in 2026. Let's break it down.

Architecture

What's Under the Hood

Gemma 4: Gemini 3 DNA, Open Weights

Google describes Gemma 4 as sharing architecture and training methodology with Gemini 3. This isn't a distilled or compressed version — it's trained on the same research pipeline as Google's best proprietary model, then released under Apache 2.0. The family spans four tiers: edge models (E2B, E4B) engineered with Qualcomm and MediaTek for on-device NPU inference, a 26B MoE that activates only 3.8B parameters per token, and a 31B dense flagship.

The MoE design on the 26B is particularly interesting from a quantization standpoint. Active-parameter efficiency means the memory bandwidth cost per token is dramatically lower than the 26B total parameter count suggests. This also means per-layer activation statistics change dynamically with routing — which matters a lot if you're applying AWQ or GPTQ calibration on top.

GPT-5.5: First Full Retrain Since GPT-4.5

OpenAI has been explicit about this: every model from GPT-5.0 through GPT-5.4 was post-training iteration on the same base. GPT-5.5 is a ground-up retrain. Three things genuinely changed at the architectural level. First, natively omnimodal — text, images, audio, and video in a single unified model, not a pipeline of separate specialists stitched together. Second, co-designed with NVIDIA's GB200/GB300 NVL72 rack-scale systems, enabling inference optimization that prior models couldn't achieve. Third, a unified router that auto-switches between Instant and Thinking modes based on query complexity.

For quantization researchers: GPT-5.5 is API-only — you cannot inspect weights, apply post-training quantization, or run it locally. Gemma 4's open weights mean the entire PTQ toolchain (GPTQ, AWQ, SpinQuant) is available. For on-device deployment research, there is no comparison: Gemma 4 is the only option.

Capabilities

Where Each Model Actually Wins

Agentic Coding: GPT-5.5, and It's Not Close

GPT-5.5 was specifically designed around Codex's agentic coding workloads. The 82.7% Terminal-Bench 2.0 score, the internal Expert-SWE at 73.1% (tasks with a 20-hour median human completion time), the 40% token efficiency improvement over GPT-5.4 — these are not benchmark numbers OpenAI optimized for. Teams at OpenAI are using it internally at 85%+ weekly penetration across software engineering, finance, and comms. Early CodeRabbit testing shows GPT-5.5 improving expected issue find rate from 55% to 65% in real code review. This is the model's genuine strong suit.

On-Device and Edge Deployment: Gemma 4, No Contest

GPT-5.5 doesn't run locally. Full stop. Gemma 4 E2B and E4B were co-engineered with Qualcomm and MediaTek for NPU-accelerated inference on Snapdragon and MediaTek chipsets — which means better hardware acceleration out-of-the-box than any previous open model at these sizes. For Android on-device AI pipelines, edge inference, air-gapped deployments, or any scenario where you can't send data to OpenAI's API, Gemma 4 is your only serious option.

Multimodality: Genuinely Different Approaches

GPT-5.5 is natively omnimodal — text, images, audio, and video in a single end-to-end model. Gemma 4 covers text, images, and video across all four sizes, plus native audio input on the E2B and E4B edge models (which is a significant deal for on-device speech pipelines). The key difference: GPT-5.5's unified architecture processes all modalities jointly. Gemma 4's multimodality is strong but the architecture details are less clear without a full technical report.

Context Window: GPT-5.5 Wins at Scale

GPT-5.5 ships with a 1M token context window in the API. Gemma 4's server models (26B MoE, 31B Dense) have 256K; the edge models get 128K. For loading entire codebases, long research corpora, or large document workflows — GPT-5.5 has a meaningful advantage. Gemma 4's 256K is still excellent for most real-world use cases, but the ceiling difference matters for certain long-context agentic pipelines.

The Real Question

This Isn't a Race — It's Two Different Products

Most "model comparison" posts treat this as a horse race where only one model can win. That framing is wrong for Gemma 4 vs GPT-5.5, because these models are optimized for fundamentally different deployment scenarios.

GPT-5.5 is built to be the best possible model you can call via API — period. It's the engine for OpenAI's super-app ambition. The agentic coding, knowledge work, and computer use capabilities are real and well ahead of anything else if your deployment is a cloud API call.

Gemma 4 is built to be the best possible model you can own. Run it on your hardware. Quantize it. Fine-tune it. Deploy it offline. Ship it to a phone. The Apache 2.0 license removes the last friction for enterprise and sovereign deployments. No usage ceiling, no data sent to external APIs, no vendor lock-in.

The one question that settles it: Does your deployment require the weights to live on your infrastructure? If yes — Gemma 4, not even close. If no — what's your hallucination tolerance, and what's your token budget? Those two variables should drive the rest of the analysis.
Decision Guide

Who Should Use Which

Choose Gemma 4 if you are…
  • Building on-device Android / iOS apps
  • Deploying in air-gapped or sovereign environments
  • Running quantization experiments (W3/W4 PTQ)
  • Working on research that needs fine-tuning access
  • Building for markets with 140+ language coverage
  • Cost-sensitive at high token volumes
  • Building on Qualcomm / MediaTek NPU stacks
  • Needing commercial deployment without usage caps
Choose GPT-5.5 if you are…
  • Building agentic coding or developer tooling
  • Running multi-step computer-use workflows
  • Needing 1M+ token long-context pipelines
  • Doing knowledge work at frontier capability levels
  • Already integrated in the ChatGPT / Codex ecosystem
  • Running financial modeling or investment banking workflows
  • Prioritizing raw benchmark performance over cost
  • Can tolerate a higher hallucination rate with verification steps
Quantization Note

For Those of Us Running PTQ Experiments

I've been running W4 experiments on the Gemma 4 26B MoE this week, so a few quick observations relevant to the quantization community that you won't find in the standard model comparisons:

W4A16 INT4 weights 26B MoE — 3.8B active params Dynamic routing per token 256K context Fits dual consumer GPU at INT4

The MoE routing creates dynamic activation statistics that complicate calibration-based methods. AWQ's scale-search and GPTQ's Hessian estimation both assume relatively stable activation distributions across calibration samples — the expert routing variability breaks this assumption more than in dense models. SpinQuant's learned rotation is showing more promise here than fixed Hadamard, which makes sense given the geometry is input-dependent.

GPT-5.5 is entirely irrelevant to this discussion. Black-box API. No weight access. If you're doing PTQ research, Gemma 4 is your only playground in the frontier tier.

Quantization researchers specifically: The Gemma 4 31B Dense in bfloat16 fits on a single 80 GB H100. At W4, comfortably fits on a single consumer GPU with 24 GB VRAM. The 26B MoE at INT4 fits dual consumer GPU setups. Both are immediately runnable. The full PTQ toolchain — AWQ, GPTQ, SpinQuant, QuaRot — is available day one. That's not a marginal advantage over a closed API; it's the difference between doing research and not doing research.
Verdict

Final Thoughts

If I had to summarize this in two sentences: GPT-5.5 is the best thing you can call via API for complex, agentic, long-horizon work. Gemma 4 is the best thing you can own — and the gap between "owning" a frontier-class model and "renting" one is larger than most organizations have fully internalized.

The hallucination number on GPT-5.5 (86% vs. Opus 4.7's 36% on independent evals) is the one result I'd push back hardest on in OpenAI's framing. A model that scores highest on the intelligence index but hallucinates at more than twice the competitor rate is not straightforwardly the best choice for production knowledge work. Dial in verification steps and it may well be — but that's additional engineering overhead that the benchmark card doesn't account for.

Gemma 4 winning Apache 2.0 licensing, edge NPU support, quantization access, and cost — that's a real moat for a specific and large class of real-world deployments. The open model story has never been this compelling at the frontier tier.
