01 — Can Money Buy Happiness?

Yes — Until
Comparison Walks In

Remember your first salary? Mine was small, but it didn't matter. With that cheque came something money can't quite name — freedom. You stop asking your parents for pocket money. That feeling? That is money buying happiness.

So money does buy happiness. Until one specific thing enters the room: comparison. Imagine you and a colleague finish the same project. You find out later they got a bigger bonus for it. The money in your account hasn\'t changed — but how you feel about it completely has. Now it carries ego, envy, insecurity.

Here is a choice worth testing on yourself. Offered two worlds and asked to pick, most people still choose to be poorer in absolute terms — as long as they out-earned their friend. Try it yourself.

There are two ways to hold money. One: a tool to live a better life. Two: a measuring tape to check if you're ahead of the people around you. The second one is quietly poisonous — especially if you grew up middle class.

Think of the uncle in your extended family who lived small his whole life, then finally built his own house and now throws a party every weekend. There is nothing wrong with that pride. He earned it. No influencer's spreadsheet can price the feeling of moving from a rented room into your own home.

Money buys real happiness — right up until you start using it to keep score against your friends. The number that makes you richer can also make you miserable.
02 — The More Trap

The 4th Coffee
Ruins the Day

Economics students remember one law till their last breath, the way science students remember "mitochondria is the powerhouse of the cell." It is the Law of Diminishing Marginal Utility. And every brand on earth is built to make you forget it.

Think about your morning coffee. The first cup is everything — clarity, warmth, the start of the day. The second is still good. By the third you\'re just going through the motions. The fourth leaves you jittery and worse off than before. Each extra cup gives you less, until satisfaction flips negative. Drag the slider and watch it happen.

So how do brands keep selling you the 4th coffee? One word: variety. Sneaker labels drop the same shoe in a new colour every few months. Watchmakers release a "limited edition" at double the price. Phone makers ship a new model for marginal gains. Novelty resets the satisfaction meter — artificially.

You have heard the stories — closets full of sneakers that never touch the ground, parcels arriving daily that never get opened. At that point you are not buying things. You are buying a hit of dopamine — and the brands are squeezing you for it.

Money is a great servant but a terrible master. The moment you're chasing the next purchase instead of using money to live better, the money is using you.
03 — Thoughtful Spending

The Things Worth
Spending On Don't
Need a Reason

The real problem today isn't that we spend too much. It's that we spend and still feel nothing. The new thing is exciting for exactly one day, then it's just another object. So how do you spend money and actually keep the happiness?

Here is the test I use. Each of us has 2–3 things we love that need no justification to anyone. Mine is cars. I bought a BMW X3 — not as an "asset", not for a reel. I bought it because I love to drive, and because I wanted my parents and my family to sit in a car that feels like comfort and care.

A finance guru will tell you: "Had you bought shares of that car company 20 years ago instead, you'd be a crorepati." That advice is broken for two reasons. One — I need a car to drive and to take my family out; a share certificate can't do that. This is a utility decision, not a financial one. Two — if everyone bought the shares instead of the cars, the company would sell nothing and the share would go nowhere.

So before you buy, run the expense through two honest questions. They split every purchase into a love expense worth spending on, or a status expense that's really just performing. Try it on something you're tempted to buy right now.

Be brutally honest — the test only works that way.

And the second half of thoughtful spending: show off the inside of the house, not the outside. Real wealth, the old line goes, is a calm mind, a healthy body, and a home full of love — none of it buyable. The luxury logo? Counterfeit bags have become so convincing these days that even shop staff struggle to tell them from the real thing. If the badge is that easy to fake, you are paying almost the whole price for the logo. Spend on what brings your people closer instead.

You're allowed 2–3 expenses that need no spreadsheet justification — the things you genuinely love. Spend on them without guilt, within your budget. Everything else, ask: am I buying this, or performing it?
04 — Finance Is Personal

There Is No
Universal Rule

Here is a sharp point worth sitting with: most financial advice on the internet is useless to you. Not because the information is wrong — but because it wasn't built for you, your goals, or your risk appetite.

The internet sells fixed rules. Buy this, rent that, never do FD, max out equity. But money decisions should be driven by your values. If you're a saver by nature, stash more each month. If you live for experiences, spend on travel and save what's left. Both are completely valid. Where do you sit?

"No FD, ever" is a great line in a bull market. But look around: when short-term mutual fund returns turn negative, the FD's modest return is at least positive. Yes, FDs are low. But timing is a massive angle that no spreadsheet captures — and a five-month-old influencer who only saw the market rally won't warn you about it.

When you reduce personal finance to an Excel sheet, you forget that you are a human being with a specific life, specific people, and a specific moment in time. That context is the whole game.

Personal finance is more personal than it is finance. What's right for me may be completely wrong for you. You earned the money. How you spend it is yours to decide.
05 — Spending vs Performing

Am I Spending —
Or Performing?

Here is a money rule I keep coming back to: spend extravagantly on the few things you truly love, as long as you cut mercilessly on the things you don't. The hard part is knowing which is which. There's a one-question filter for that.

Once it was a coffee cup in every photo. Then a phone in one very specific colour. Then a viral water bottle, then a snack everyone filmed themselves trying. I've never met anyone who bought that snack for the taste — they bought it for the post. That's the tell. Before any purchase, ask one thing: am I spending this on my life, or performing it for an audience?

On one side, finance influencers push saving to an extreme. On the other, lifestyle influencers take loans to flaunt a life they can't afford. Both manufacture FOMO. As a viewer you end up confused — the internet educates less and irritates more. The fix is to build your own comfort zone and stop optimising for anyone else's feed.

And one rule sits above all of this: never spend money you don't have. Taking an EMI for a want is a trap dressed up as access. The freedom is in being able to pay — and choosing how.

Before you tap "buy", ask the only question that matters: am I spending, or am I performing? If the audience is the reason, it isn't yours — it's theirs.
The Playbook

How To Actually
Spend Money

None of this is complicated. Stop optimising for a spreadsheet and start optimising for your life. Five frameworks — that's the whole book in your pocket.

Framework 01
Money buys happiness — kill comparison
The freedom of your own money is real joy. The moment you use it to keep score against friends, it turns into ego and envy. Spend to live better, not to win a race.
Framework 02
Respect diminishing returns
The 4th coffee ruins the day. Brands sell "variety" and "limited editions" to reset a meter that's already full. More is the root of the stress. Notice when the money starts using you.
Framework 03
Spend on what you love — guilt-free
Keep 2–3 expenses that need no justification. Would you buy it if no one saw it? Does it bring your people closer? Then it's a love expense. Show off the inside of the house, not the logo.
Framework 04
Finance is personal — own your rule
No universal blanket statement fits everyone. Saver or experiencer, both work. Most internet advice isn't wrong — it just wasn't built for you. Decide by your values and your timing.
Framework 05
Spend extravagantly, cut mercilessly
Make your list of 10 wants. Spend freely on the top 5. Be stingy on the bottom 5. Before every purchase: am I spending, or performing? And never, ever spend money you don't have.
