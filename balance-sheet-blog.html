Part 01

How to Read a Balance Sheet

A practical walkthrough using HDFC Bank's FY2026 results.

A balance sheet is just one idea repeated in a lot of detail: what a company owns, what it owes, and what's left over for shareholders. That's it. Everything else is just breaking those three buckets into finer categories.

Bank balance sheets look scarier than a regular company's because banks don't sell products — they buy and sell money. But once you know what to look for, they're actually easier to read than, say, a manufacturing company's, because there's less inventory and supply-chain noise to wade through.

We'll use HDFC Bank's results for the year ended March 31, 2026 (FY26) — India's largest private bank, and a good example precisely because it's simple, well-run, and has clean disclosures.

01The One Equation That Explains Everything

Assets = Liabilities + Equity
the whole balance sheet, in one line
  • Assets — everything the bank owns or is owed (loans it has given out, investments, cash)
  • Liabilities — everything it owes to others (deposits, borrowings)
  • Equity — what's left for shareholders if you paid off every liability

For a bank, flip your intuition: deposits are a liability, not an asset. When you put money in a savings account, the bank owes you that money — it's sitting on the bank's balance sheet as a debt. The bank's real "assets" are the loans it makes with your money.

03The Liabilities Side: Where Does the Money Come From?

For a bank, the liabilities side answers: "Whose money is this, and how expensive/sticky is it?"

a) Deposits — the core funding source

HDFC Bank's total deposits stood at ₹31,053 billion as of March 31, 2026, growing 14.4% year-on-year, with gross advances of ₹29,600 billion, up 12.0%, and CASA deposits making up 34.1% of the total.

Why CASA matters: CASA = Current Account + Savings Account deposits. These pay little to no interest, unlike fixed/term deposits. A higher CASA ratio means cheaper funding, which supports margins. HDFC Bank's CASA ratio has been gently declining post-merger (from 34.8% to 34.1%) as term deposits have grown faster — worth watching, but not alarming at this level.

b) Borrowings

The bank's borrowings as a share of total liabilities fell to about 11% by March 2026, described as a structural improvement that lowers refinancing risk and funding costs. This matters because after HDFC Bank merged with its parent HDFC Ltd. (a housing-finance company) in mid-2023, it inherited a lot of relatively costlier wholesale borrowings. Steadily replacing those with deposits is a sign the post-merger integration is maturing.

c) Equity / Shareholders' Funds

This is the cushion that absorbs losses before depositors or lenders are at risk. You gauge its adequacy not from the rupee number alone but from the Capital Adequacy Ratio (CAR) — equity and other qualifying capital, expressed as a percentage of risk-weighted assets. HDFC Bank's CAR stood at 19.7% as of March 31, 2026, comfortably above the RBI's regulatory requirement of 11.9%, with a CET1 (core equity) ratio of 17.3%.

Rule of thumb: for Indian banks, CAR above ~15% is comfortable; HDFC Bank at ~19.7% has a wide buffer, meaning it can absorb shocks and still has room to grow its loan book without urgently raising fresh capital.

04The Assets Side: What Is the Money Being Used For?

a) Advances (loans) — the main event

Gross advances reached ₹29,600 billion as of March 31, 2026, up 12.0% over the prior year, with retail loans up 6.5%, small and mid-market enterprise loans up 17.2%, and corporate/wholesale loans up 13.0%.

Notice the mix shift: retail (home loans, personal loans, credit cards) grew the slowest, while SME and corporate lending grew fastest. That's a meaningful read on strategy — post-merger, HDFC Bank has been diversifying away from being purely a retail-heavy lender.

b) Investments

Banks don't lend out 100% of their deposits — regulation requires them to hold a portion in government securities (the SLR, or Statutory Liquidity Ratio) for safety and liquidity. This shows up as "Investments" on the balance sheet, separate from advances.

c) Cash and balances with RBI

Every bank must also maintain a CRR (Cash Reserve Ratio) — a slice of deposits parked with the central bank, earning no interest. It looks like "dead money" but it's a regulatory requirement, not a management choice.

05The Health Checks: NPAs and Provisioning

This is where a balance sheet stops being an accounting exercise and starts telling you whether the lending book is actually good.

  • Gross NPA ratio = bad loans ÷ total loans. HDFC Bank's stood at 1.15% as of March 31, 2026, improved from 1.33% a year earlier.
  • Net NPA ratio (after provisioning for expected losses) was 0.38% as of March 31, 2026.
  • Provisions for the quarter fell to ₹2,609.57 crore in Q4FY26, down 18% year-on-year, with the total credit cost ratio at 0.35% for the quarter.

Why this matters more than the headline profit number: a bank can show a great profit in a quarter by simply not setting aside enough money for loans that might go bad. Falling provisions alongside falling NPAs (as here) is the good version of this story — it means the loan book is genuinely getting cleaner, not that management is being less careful. If provisions had fallen while NPAs rose, that would be a red flag.

06Putting It Together: A Five-Step Reading Method

Here's the checklist to apply to any bank balance sheet, not just HDFC Bank's:

  1. Start with the size and growth rate. Is the balance sheet growing, and is that growth funded by deposits (healthy) or borrowings (needs scrutiny)?
  2. Check the deposit mix (CASA vs term). Higher CASA = cheaper, stickier funding.
  3. Compare deposit growth to loan growth. If loans are growing faster than deposits for several quarters, the bank is either running down liquid assets or leaning on borrowings — both increase risk.
  4. Look at CAR and CET1. This tells you how much of a buffer exists before shareholders and then depositors would be at risk.
  5. Check NPA trend, not just the level. A stable or improving GNPA/NNPA ratio, alongside adequate provisioning, is the real test of loan-book quality — not the profit number alone.

Run HDFC Bank's FY26 numbers through this checklist and the picture is consistent: balance sheet growing at a healthy ~12%, deposits outpacing loans, borrowings shrinking as a share of funding, capital comfortably above requirement, and asset quality improving. That's what a bank balance sheet "in good health" looks like.

07A Few Caveats Worth Keeping in Mind

The HDFC Ltd. merger (July 2023) makes year-over-year comparisons before FY24 unreliable — the bank itself flags this in every results release. Stick to FY24 onward for clean comparisons.
Standalone vs. consolidated: the numbers above are standalone (the bank alone). Consolidated figures include subsidiaries like HDFC Life, HDFC AMC, HDFC ERGO, and HDB Financial Services, and will show a different profit and balance sheet picture.
A balance sheet is a snapshot, not a movie. It tells you the position on one date (March 31, 2026, here). Always read it alongside the profit & loss statement and cash flow statement for the full picture of how the bank got there.

Figures sourced from HDFC Bank's Q4 FY26 and full-year results (announced April 18, 2026) and related regulatory filings.

STRONG Capital Adequacy Ratio at 19.7%, well above the 11.9% regulatory floor — ample room to keep growing the loan book.
WATCH CASA ratio has drifted down from 34.8% to 34.1% — funding is getting slightly less cheap as term deposits grow faster.
WATCH Leadership transition — a part-time chairman vacancy following a March 2026 resignation is an open governance item the market may be discounting for.

How to read this page, honestly: a low P/E next to improving fundamentals is a pattern worth investigating further — it is not, by itself, a buy signal. Multiples can stay low for a long time, re-rate for reasons unrelated to fundamentals, or reflect risks (like the governance item above) that a balance sheet doesn't capture. Use this alongside the full balance sheet write-up above, the bank's own disclosures, and independent research — and treat any investment decision as your own, made with full awareness of your risk tolerance and time horizon.
