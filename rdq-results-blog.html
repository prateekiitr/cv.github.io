The Numbers Are In: RDQ, Residual Drift, and What Actually Happens Below 4-Bit

A follow-up to "The Rotation Trick That Separates Good Quantization from Great." CEC's real numbers across three architectures, the ablation that answers "isn't this just AWQ with different data," and an honest update on the RotRDQ hypothesis I floated last time.

← Part 1: The Rotation Trick That Separates Good Quantization from Great

Last post I mapped the field — GPTQ, AWQ, QuaRot, SpinQuant — and left two things open. First, an intuition about CEC that I hadn't yet backed with numbers: "CEC minimizes cumulative error — it asks what codebook produces the best output given the drifted inputs this layer will actually receive." Second, the RotRDQ hypothesis: rotate first to kill outliers, then apply CEC to clean up the residual drift.

The Gemma 4 MoE run is still going. But the RDQ paper is done — full experiments across three dense architectures, two bit-widths, against every baseline from Part 1. Here's what held up, what surprised me, and where RotRDQ actually stands.

The Mechanism, Tightened

Why 3-Bit Breaks: It's Not the Outliers This Time

Part 1 was about outliers — a few channels with values 100–1000× the median, destroying resolution when squeezed into 16 buckets. That's a real failure mode, and rotation fixes it well. But on LLaMA-3-8B, RTN-128 goes from 5.83 PPL at W4 to 14.09 PPL at W3 — a 2.42× perplexity hit for only 1.33× more compression. Outliers alone don't explain a cliff that steep.

The residual stream h is shared across every layer — each one reads from it and writes back to it. Quantize layer l, and its error εl doesn't stay local. It gets carried into hl+1, and every downstream layer inherits it on top of whatever error it introduces itself:

ĥl = hl + Σj<l εj
// layer l's real input, drifted by every upstream quantization error
// standard PTQ calibrates against h^l, not ĥ^l — a distribution that no longer exists at inference
The single-number payoff: total KL drift from the FP16 baseline correlates with log-perplexity at Pearson r = 0.999 (p < 0.001), across every method and bit-width tested. Quantization quality isn't a black box — it's predictable from one scalar, and that scalar is controllable layer-by-layer.
Diagnosis

Three Things the Residual Stream Was Doing That Nobody Was Measuring

r = 0.999 drift ↔ log-PPL σ² grows 6,548× across depth 27 / 32 layers fail KS normality 84% non-Gaussian residuals

Two findings sharpen the diagnosis. Per-layer residual variance grows 6,548× from early to late layers — early layers sit around σ² ≈ 10⁻⁴, late layers reach ≈ 0.65. Later layers aren't just "further along the network," they're categorically more fragile to whatever error arrives from upstream. That's exactly where a drift-correcting method should earn its keep.

The second finding is the one I didn't expect going in: 27 of 32 layers fail a Kolmogorov–Smirnov normality test. Only six layers — {1, 7, 20, 26, 28, 30} — look Gaussian. Non-Gaussianity is the rule, not the exception, and it quietly invalidates the Gaussian assumption baked into most PTQ theory, including my own perplexity bound (more on that below).

CEC

Cascaded Error Compensation, Now With Numbers

The intuition from Part 1 turned into a five-step pipeline:

  • 1️⃣
    Quantize layers 0..l−1 in sequence, not independently.
  • 2️⃣
    Capture layer l's real inputs via forward_pre_hook — the drifted activations it will actually see, not clean FP16 ones. First 8 tokens per sequence are BOS-filtered.
  • 3️⃣
    Fit AWQ-style per-channel scales against those drifted inputs — a 20-point grid search over α minimizing real output reconstruction error.
  • 4️⃣
    Fold the scale into the preceding RMSNorm — mathematically exact, zero inference overhead, no custom kernel.
  • 5️⃣
    Move to layer l+1, which now sees inputs drifted by everything quantized so far.

The RMSNorm fold is the detail I like most — it's not an approximation:

x / RMS(x) · (w / s) · (W · s) = x / RMS(x) · w · W = LNold(x) · W ✓
// scale and inverse-scale cancel exactly — same forward pass, same latency, same kernels

There's also a safety fallback: α = 0 (plain RTN, no scaling) is always tested as a baseline per layer. If no α > 0 beats it, CEC keeps s = 1. It fires in about 12% of layer-groups — mostly where RTN already fits the drifted distribution well enough. The other 88% is where the AWQ-style scaling is doing real work, at a mean active α ≈ 0.42.

The Obvious Objection

"Isn't This Just AWQ With Different Calibration Data?"

Yes — and that's kind of the point. Table 3 in the paper is built specifically to answer this, on Qwen-2.5-7B at W3A16, where AWQ has its worst showing:

Method W3 PPL What's different
AWQ (static FP16 calibration) 9.491 Independent layers, clean inputs
RTN-128 (no scaling) 8.199 No calibration at all
CEC (α=0, sequential only) 10.310 Drifted calibration, no AWQ scaling
RDQ + CEC (both) 7.462 Both components

The AWQ scaling formula is identical in row 1 and row 4. The only difference is whether the calibration data reflects what the layer actually receives at inference. That gap — 9.49 → 7.46 — is entirely drift-aware calibration.

The surprise: sequential calibration alone, without AWQ scaling (row 3), is worse than plain RTN — 10.31 vs 8.20. Re-calibrating on drifted inputs without also improving the per-channel dynamic range just disturbs the RTN grid. Neither piece works alone; you need drift-aware inputs and the scaling search together.

There's also an architecture-sensitivity story here. AWQ's salient-channel heuristic — E|X| on clean FP16 activations — was effectively derived from LLaMA-family statistics, and it mis-identifies important channels on Qwen's different weight distribution (AWQ W4 gives 8.50 on Qwen vs RDQ's 6.38 — a 2.11 PPL gap). CEC sidesteps this because its importance metric is computed on whatever the layer actually sees, which makes it distribution-agnostic by construction.

Revisiting Part 1

So Where Does the RotRDQ Hypothesis Stand?

Last post I floated this: if outliers are severe enough to corrupt CEC's starting point, pre-rotating to eliminate them and then running CEC on the cleaner distribution should be strictly better than either alone. Honest update — the RDQ paper doesn't test this combination. It's still an open hypothesis, not a validated result.

What the paper does establish is that CEC is explicitly a wrapper, not a replacement: it's compatible with any per-layer quantizer — RTN, GPTQ, AWQ, LeanQuant — because the sequential drift-correction loop is orthogonal to the per-layer optimization itself. That's the same property that would make RotRDQ possible in principle: rotate first for geometry, then let CEC run its sequential capture-and-fit loop against the rotated (and still drifting) residual stream. Nothing in the CEC mechanism assumes un-rotated inputs.

Updating the decision map from Part 1 with what's now measured vs. still open:

  • 🔴
    Outliers dominate, drift is mild? Rotation (QuaRot/SpinQuant) — unchanged from Part 1, now with no new evidence either way from the RDQ experiments.
  • 🟢
    Drift dominates, outliers are mild? CEC alone — now backed by measured numbers: −46.4% vs RTN at W3 on LLaMA, SOTA on 6/6 cells tested.
  • 🟠
    Both are severe? RotRDQ — theoretically composable (CEC is quantizer-agnostic by design), but untested. This is the natural next experiment, not a confirmed result.
  • 🟣
    MoE architecture? Still unresolved — Gemma 4 26B experiments are ongoing, see below.
Where the Theory Is Weaker Than the Numbers

The Gaussian Bound Doesn't Actually Hold

The paper includes a perplexity bound derived under a Gaussian-residual assumption: PPL_quant ≤ PPL_fp16 · exp(K√(2·D_total)). It predicts a 1.06× degradation at W3 on LLaMA-3-8B. The measured degradation is 1.36×. The bound is violated, not conservative — which is exactly what you'd expect once you know 84% of layers fail the Gaussian normality test. I've kept this in the paper as a labeled proposition rather than a theorem for that reason; it's directionally right (more drift → worse PPL) but not a tight guarantee at 3-bit.

Takeaway

Updated One-Paragraph Summary

Part 1's framework still holds: GPTQ corrects error locally via Hessian sensitivity, AWQ protects vulnerable channels via scaling, and rotation methods (QuaRot, SpinQuant) eliminate outliers geometrically before quantization ever happens. RDQ's Cascaded Error Compensation addresses the piece none of those touch — error that accumulates across the depth of the network, correlated with log-perplexity at r=0.999, growing 6,548× across layers, and not covered by a Gaussian-regime theory that turns out to hold for only 16% of the model. On three architectures and two bit-widths, CEC beats the best published baseline at every cell tested, at zero inference overhead. Whether it stacks with rotation is still an open question.
